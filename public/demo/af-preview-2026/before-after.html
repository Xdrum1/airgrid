What changes when you evaluate whether a site is actually operable — not just approved.

Most aviation facility assessments rely on FAA records. The challenge is that those records are often incomplete or outdated. AirIndex structures the evaluation to account for that — before decisions are made.
The data problem
The FAA system was not designed for this level of evaluation or this scale of deployment. 99% of heliports are privately owned — the FAA is not required to verify whether their data is current. 40% were built before the FAA established the dimensional terminology still in use today — meaning the pad dimensions in the record may not represent what current standards define. Decisions are being made on top of this data.

Why this matters
In one documented case, a heliport was recorded as 100×100 ft in FAA data. The actual landing area was 40×40 ft — and obstructions penetrated required safety surfaces. The incident was attributed to pilot error. The underlying infrastructure issues were not captured in the data. For facilities built before 1994, even accurately recorded dimensions may represent different physical measurements than current terminology defines. This is exactly the type of discrepancy we aim to surface before it reaches the underwriting file.

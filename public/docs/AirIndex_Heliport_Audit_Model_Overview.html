The Problem We're Solving

Every state, city, and airport authority planning for Advanced Air Mobility needs to answer one question first: what vertical flight infrastructure do we already have?

The answer should be simple. It's not. As your VFS Forum 80 research documented, the FAA's Airport Master Record has significant data quality issues:

  • Facilities that no longer exist are still listed as active in the system
  • Location coordinates can be miles off from the actual facility (the TN60 example: 55.95 statute miles off)
  • Emergency Helicopter Landing Facilities (EHLFs) are mixed in with operational heliports, inflating counts in cities like Los Angeles and San Francisco
  • Dimensional data is unreliable — heliport TLOF/FATO dimensions are recorded using airport runway fields, with no heliport-specific data elements
  • Thousands of facilities are missing entirely — NASA estimates 2,000+ hospital heliports alone are not in the FAA system
  • Standards compliance is unknown — no way to determine from FAA data whether a facility meets current AC 150/5390-2D requirements

The result: decision-makers working from incomplete data, building plans on a foundation they can't trust. States that think they have zero existing infrastructure might actually have dozens of conversion-ready sites.

What the Audit Model Does

The AirIndex audit model takes every available data source for a given state or metro area, cross-references them to build a complete picture, and flags discrepancies that need physical verification. Think of it as a three-stage process:

1
Aggregate
Pull every known facility from FAA 5010, OE/AAA obstruction filings, state DOT aviation databases, hospital helipad registries, and landing zone databases. Combine them into a single inventory for the target area.
2
Cross-Reference & Flag
Match facilities across sources by geographic proximity. When a facility appears in one source but not another, flag the discrepancy. When coordinates disagree, flag it. When a facility is listed as active in FAA records but doesn't appear in any other source, flag it as a potential ghost. When a facility appears in landing zone or hospital data but not in FAA records, flag it as potentially undocumented.
3
Score & Prioritize
For every confirmed facility, calculate a Conversion Potential Score — how suitable is this heliport for future vertiport conversion? Rank all facilities so the buyer knows which sites to prioritize for physical evaluation.

Key point: The digital audit doesn't replace physical verification. It tells you where to look and what to look for, so physical site evaluations are targeted and efficient rather than starting from scratch.

Conversion Potential Score

Every facility in the audit inventory receives a Conversion Potential Score based on six factors. This score answers the question: if someone wanted to convert this heliport into a vertiport, how viable is it?

Factor 1
Ownership Type
Public-use facilities have clearer regulatory pathways for conversion than private-use. Public ownership typically means fewer zoning hurdles.
Factor 2
Facility Classification
Is it a true heliport, a helipad, or an EHLF? Each has different conversion implications. True heliports with established operations are strongest candidates.
Factor 3
Physical Dimensions
Pad size, FATO dimensions (where reliable), and elevation. Larger facilities with adequate safety areas have more conversion flexibility. Higher elevations may need additional FATO length per AC 150/5390-2D.
Factor 4
Location & Demand
Proximity to population centers, airports, hospitals, business districts, and transit hubs. A heliport near a central business district or major airport scores higher than one in an isolated area.
Factor 5
Airspace & Zoning
Airspace classification, obstruction environment, and local zoning compatibility. Facilities with established airspace determinations and favorable zoning have a head start.
Factor 6
Market Readiness
How ready is the surrounding metro market for AAM? This pulls from the AirIndex Readiness Score — operator presence, legislation, regulatory posture, and corridor proximity all factor in.

Each factor is scored and weighted to produce an overall Conversion Potential rating:

Rating What It Means Recommended Action
HIGH Strong conversion candidate. Favorable ownership, adequate dimensions, good location, supportive regulatory environment. Priority candidate for physical site evaluation and feasibility study.
MODERATE Viable with modifications. May need dimensional upgrades, zoning changes, or operational adjustments. Include in planning inventory. Evaluate if modifications are cost-effective.
LOW Significant barriers to conversion. May be an EHLF, have restrictive zoning, or lack adequate dimensions. Document for completeness. Not a near-term conversion target.
VERIFY Insufficient or conflicting data. Cannot score accurately until physical verification confirms facility status and dimensions. Needs on-site evaluation before any planning decisions are made.

Discrepancy Detection

The audit model's cross-referencing process surfaces five types of discrepancies, each drawn directly from the data quality issues documented in your VFS Forum 80 research:

Discrepancy Type What It Means Example
Ghost Facility Listed in FAA 5010 as active but not confirmed by any other source. May no longer exist. Heliport with no operations data, no OE/AAA filings, no satellite confirmation, and an owner information date over 20 years old.
Undocumented Facility Active facility found in landing zone data, hospital registries, or obstruction filings but not in FAA 5010. Hospital heliport in active air ambulance use per landing zone database but missing from FAA Airport Master Record.
Location Mismatch Coordinates in FAA records don't match coordinates from other sources or satellite imagery. Recorded coordinates place the facility miles from its actual location (per your TN60 Nashville finding: 55.95 miles off).
Classification Conflict Facility categorized differently across sources. Particularly common with EHLFs counted as heliports. Building EHLF in LA (required for structures over 100') listed as a heliport in FAA records but never used for helicopter operations.
Dimensional Discrepancy Recorded dimensions don't match physical site or other sources. FAA records show 100' x 100' (the garage deck) when actual TLOF is 40' x 40' (per your Tucson Medical Center AZ37 finding).

Data Sources

The model draws from multiple public and semi-public sources. No single source is complete — the value is in the cross-reference:

Source What It Contributes Limitation
FAA 5010
Airport Master Record		 Baseline inventory. Name, location, ownership, use type, dimensions, activation date, operations data. Incomplete, inaccurate coordinates, no EHLF distinction, stale private-use data. Your research: ~19% error rate in tested states.
FAA OE/AAA
Obstruction Filings		 Airspace determinations for proposed or modified facilities. Surfaces facilities that filed for airspace review even if not in 5010. Only covers facilities that went through the obstruction evaluation process.
Landing Zone
Databases		 Active helicopter landing zones verified by air medical crews. Photos, GPS coordinates, hazard information. Covers facilities FAA doesn't track. Focused on air ambulance operations. May not cover corporate or industrial facilities.
State DOT
Aviation Databases		 State-level airport and heliport inventories. Quality varies — some states (California for hospital heliports) maintain good records. Not all states maintain separate databases. Format and completeness vary widely.
AirIndex
Market Intelligence		 Market readiness scores, operator presence, corridor proximity, regulatory posture, legislation status for the surrounding metro area. Market-level context, not facility-level. Used for the "Market Readiness" scoring factor.

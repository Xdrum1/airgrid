Page 2 — What Shows Up at the Facility Level

Below the Market Score

Market readiness scores reflect regulatory and operator conditions. They do not capture what happens at the individual facility. The following patterns represent constraints that do not appear in market-level scoring but directly impact operational feasibility at the site level. Across the 368 heliports in these five metros:

Dimensional compliance does not mean operational viability. Many facilities meet minimum pad dimensions but have constrained FATO zones, limited expansion space, and approach surfaces that would require variance under current Advisory Circular standards for larger airframes.
Rooftop airflow exposure is not captured in registry data. Hospital and urban rooftop heliports face thermal updraft, HVAC exhaust proximity, and recirculation effects from surrounding structures. None of this appears in FAA facility records. It must be assessed independently.
Pre-1994 facilities create interpretation ambiguity. Over 40% of U.S. heliports were built before the current dimensional terminology (TLOF, FATO, Safety Area) was standardized. Dimensional data in the registry often reflects legacy definitions that don't map cleanly to current requirements.
Obstruction environments vary significantly within the same metro. Two facilities in the same city can have completely different 8:1 approach surface profiles. One may be clear; the other may have multiple penetrations. Market-level readiness does not predict site-level operability.
Data staleness is widespread. Many facility records have not had positional or dimensional data verified in over a decade. Activation dates, surface conditions, and inspection records are frequently blank or outdated. Planning on this baseline introduces silent risk.
No market has full low-altitude weather coverage. All five markets rely on airport ASOS stations for surface weather. None have dedicated low-altitude sensing along potential AAM corridors. Weather infrastructure is the universal remaining gap, even in ADVANCED-tier markets.

For OEMs and operators, these conditions determine where existing infrastructure can realistically support next-generation aircraft without significant modification.
